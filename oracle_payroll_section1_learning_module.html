Oracle's central truth: Oracle enforces what is configured — it does not protect against misconfiguration. Payroll may calculate even when foundational setup is wrong, but compliance, tax, and reporting will eventually fail.

⬡What is the LDG?

The Legislative Data Group is the highest payroll-specific legal boundary in Oracle Fusion HCM. It is country-specific and governs which tax engine runs, which statutory rules apply, which localizations are active, and which balance types and reports exist.

Setup and Maintenance → Workforce Deployment / Payroll → Legislative Data Groups
✓What LDG controls
  • Federal, state, and local tax logic
  • Statutory deductions and contributions
  • Year-end reporting structure
  • Regulatory validation during payroll
  • Which delivered reports are available
!Hard constraints
  • Payroll objects cannot cross LDGs
  • Retro and balances do not cross LDGs
  • Errors show as reporting failures, not calc failures
  • Hard to change post go-live
  • Multicountry = separate LDG per country
?Key discovery questions
  • Are all employees governed by the same country's labor and tax laws?
  • Do we anticipate paying employees in another country within the same Oracle instance?
  • Do payroll users understand they cannot fix LDG issues downstream?
  • Who owns requests for new LDGs if expansion occurs?

PSU/TRU errors do not stop payroll calculation. Failures appear later in year-end processing, regulatory extracts, and audit reconciliation.
⬡What is the PSU?

The Payroll Statutory Unit represents the legal employer responsible for payroll compliance. It determines: who is legally responsible for payroll reports, which entity's name appears on regulatory filings, and the path for W-2, W-2C, 941, and state returns.

Setup and Maintenance → Legal Structures / Payroll → Payroll Statutory Units
⬡PSU mental model
Think of the PSU as answering:"Who is legally responsible for what payroll reports get filed, where, and under whose name?"
↔Tax Reporting Units (TRUs)

TRUs exist under a PSU and segment reporting by federal entity, state, and local jurisdiction. Employees are associated with TRUs via work location, address, and legal employer context.

✓Configuration decisions

⬡What is a Payroll Definition?

A Payroll Definition defines the execution boundary for payroll processing — who is paid together, how often, and under what cadence. Every employee must have an active payroll relationship linked to a payroll definition.

Setup and Maintenance → Payroll → Payroll Definitions
⬡What Payroll Definitions control
  • Payroll frequency and cycle (weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, monthly)
  • Pay period generation and structure
  • Retroactive behavior alignment
  • Off-cycle run eligibility
  • Reporting aggregation scope
!Frequency drives everything downstream
Payroll frequency determines:
Period structure Retro timing Accrual impact Accounting cadence Year-end complexity
✕
Employees cannot be paid if payroll relationship is missing OR if payroll definition is inactive.
?Key discovery questions
  • Do different populations require separate pay schedules or just different elements?
  • Will retiree payroll be isolated operationally?
  • How often are off-cycle payrolls realistically run?
  • Who is authorized to initiate off-cycle runs?
✓Configuration decisions
  • Active vs. retiree separation
  • Bi-weekly vs. monthly (separate definitions)
  • How off-cycle payrolls will be used
  • Naming conventions for audit/troubleshooting
  • Whether future payroll types are anticipated

⬡What is the Payroll Calendar?

Payroll calendars define when payroll is allowed to exist. They control period start/end dates, pay and check dates, and whether payroll can run at all. Period status directly controls payroll eligibility.

Setup and Maintenance → Payroll → Payroll Calendars
!Critical calendar rule
Pay attention to date types:Many processes use the pay period end date, not the check date. Finance and payroll teams must agree on which date drives calculation, reporting, and accounting.
⬡What calendars drive
  • Validity of payroll processing
  • Report parameter behavior
  • Retro placement in periods
  • Payment timing
  • Accounting period alignment
?Discovery questions
  • Which date drives calculation vs. reporting vs. accounting?
  • How are bank holidays handled today?
  • Who is allowed to reopen or adjust closed periods?
  • Do Finance and Payroll agree on date ownership?
!Configuration decisions
  • Alignment of period end vs. check date
  • Handling of bank holidays
  • Use of payment date overrides
  • Controls for opening and closing periods
  • Calendar segregation by payroll type

⚑Silent failure patterns in foundation
  • Wrong address → incorrect state/local tax with no validation
  • Missing payment method → prepayment failure without warning
  • PSU/TRU mismatch → year-end extract failure discovered in Q1
  • Misconfigured calendar → blank reports or retro in wrong period
  • Missing payroll relationship → employee not paid (no error, just absence)
Ultimate takeaway — Section 1 Payroll success in Oracle is determined long before Calculate Payroll is clicked. Foundational setup decisions are operationally silent — but legally loud. Oracle Payroll does exactly what you design: nothing more, nothing less.
